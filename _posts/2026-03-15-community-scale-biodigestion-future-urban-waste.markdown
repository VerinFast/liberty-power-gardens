Centralized waste processing has served cities for decades, but a decentralized model offers surprising advantages for density, cost, and resilience.

## The centralized problem

Most urban food waste follows a long, expensive journey: collection trucks haul it across the city to a distant processing facility or, more often, straight to a landfill. The fuel burned, the road wear, and the methane released at the dump all add up to an environmental bill that cities can no longer afford to ignore.

## Thinking smaller

Community-scale biodigesters flip this model. By processing food waste within the neighborhood that generates it, we eliminate hauling emissions, reduce infrastructure strain, and create a closed loop where the outputs — energy, soil, and clean water — flow directly back to residents.

## Resilience through distribution

A single centralized facility is a single point of failure. Distributed processing means that if one unit goes offline, the rest of the network continues. It's the same principle that makes the internet robust — and it applies just as well to waste infrastructure.
